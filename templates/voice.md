## What makes this content different

Three to seven principles. The writer skill weights these heavily. Each principle should be enforceable — a draft that violates the principle is rewritten.

Example for a B2B governance company:

> 1. **Problem-first, always.** Name the pain before the solution. The reader should feel recognized before they feel sold to.
> 2. **Capabilities, not outcomes.** Describe what the software does ("registers every agent with identity, owner, risk tier and compliance status") not what it promises ("peace of mind"). Sell the mechanism, not the result.
> 3. **Specific over general.** Name the regulation and article number. Name the survey and sample size. Name the tool the brand is replacing. Vague is the enemy.
> 4. **Honest about difficulty.** Acknowledge the reader's constraints. "This won't fix everything" builds more trust than "comprehensive solution."
> 5. **Infrastructure analogies.** Your CMDB tracks every server. Network segmentation maps data flows. AI agents deserve the same discipline.
> 6. **The "old way" is always named.** Spreadsheets. Quarterly manual audits. Slack threads. Never vague "challenges."
> 7. **E-E-A-T over commodity.** Default to the non-commodity axis: named incident, first-person decision, specific evidence, counterintuitive stance.

## Em-dash policy

Choose one:

- `allow` — em dashes permitted at standard density (max one per three paragraphs, none in title)
- `sparingly` — same as allow but the linter flags when more than one appears in a single article
- `forbidden` — no em dashes anywhere; linter errors on any

Default if not declared: `allow`.

> Policy: [allow | sparingly | forbidden]

## Oxford comma policy

Choose one:

- `ap` — AP style, no Oxford comma ("X, Y and Z")
- `chicago` — Chicago style, Oxford comma kept ("X, Y, and Z")

Default if not declared: `ap`.

> Policy: [ap | chicago]
